MENG233 Rigid Body Dynamics group project

Tendon-Driven Continuum Robot

By Erin Bilgin Çağa, Anıl Eray Özden, Mehmet Alper Fidan, İskender Baydan

Overview

⠀⠀⠀This project explores the design and prototyping of a tendon-driven continuum robot, a class of robots inspired by biological structures such as octopus arms and elephant trunks. Unlike traditional rigid-link manipulators, this robot is capable of continuous bending, allowing it to adapt its shape to the environment. The primary task of the robot is to reach, orient, and manipulate objects in confined or irregular spaces where conventional articulated robots are inefficient or unsafe. Such capabilities are especially relevant for inspection, search-and-rescue, and soft manipulation tasks where compliance and adaptability are critical.

⠀⠀

⠀⠀⠀The prototype was designed as a single-segment continuum structure driven by peripheral tendons. All structural components were 3D printed in PETG, selected for its heat resistance and durability. An infill density of 40% was used to balance structural stiffness and weight while maintaining reliable tendon guidance. To avoid restricting the robot’s curvature, the central backbone was implemented using silicone, allowing unrestricted bending without imposing a preferred shape. Tendon routing was achieved using bike brake cables, chosen for their high tensile strength, low elongation, and availability.

⠀⠀⠀Actuation was achieved using three 360-degree metal-geared servo motors, each responsible for controlling an individual tendon. A micro 180-degree metal-geared servo motor was used to actuate the end-effector gripper. All motors were selected with metal gear trains to improve durability under continuous load and repeated bending cycles. The system was controlled using an Arduino Uno, programmed in C++, enabling real-time control of tendon tension and gripper actuation.

⠀⠀⠀The entire prototype was modeled in SolidWorks prior to fabrication, allowing validation of component fit, tendon routing, and overall geometry. This modeling step reduced assembly errors and supported rapid iteration of design parameters. From a robotics perspective, this system falls within the domain of soft robotics, where compliance is intentionally introduced to improve safety, adaptability, and interaction with unstructured environments. By combining rigid printed elements with flexible silicone and tendon actuation, the robot exhibits characteristics of both continuum and soft robotic systems. This hybrid approach demonstrates how relatively simple hardware and control strategies can be used to realize complex, bio-inspired motion.
